Thanks for the bump @ ? I thought I'd answered this but evidently I hadn't. There are 3 main types of soil textural profiles: 1. Uniform ? where the texture of the soil through the profile changes very little or is the same. 2. Gradational ? where the soil texture gradually changes as you go down the profile ? often the soil particles size will get smaller as you go down 3. Duplex ? where there is a distinctive change in soil texture within the profile. Examples are where there is a lighter textured soil (e.g. sandy loam) over a heavier texture (e.g. loamy clay) Infiltration rates in duplex soils often reduce significantly as water moves vertically through the profile such that most of the water that infiltrates starts to move horizontally above the impermeable layers. This can also occur in both uniform and gradational profiles where the subsoil becomes resistant to infiltration. Where a dam is built in these conditions, and with #KeylinePatternCultivation significantly increasing infiltration rates at the same time as reducing run-off, all of the extra water that has infiltrated now finds itself entering the soil and is always finding the path of least resistance. Accordingly this water, now moving horizontally above the impermeable layers, will emerge in a dam if this dam has cut through these layers. This is why its always important to understand what your soil profiles look like. Hiring an excavator or back-hoe for a few hours can give you an incredible insight as to what the profile looks like and how it behaves under various climatic conditions. Its why when building a dam we always recommend some pit testing with an experienced person OR employing the services of an experienced Geotechnical Engineer.

Thanks for the reminder that I'd missed this one @. Reactive clay soils (Vertisols - also sometimes also called self-mulching soils) have a couple of attributes which are worth noting. 1. Very high cation exchange capacities (CEC or TEC) 2. High Magnesium:Calcium ratios Therefore if you're going to overcome the physical outcomes of this chemistry then you can only do this by adjusting the soil chemistry or soil moisture levels. Both being expensive in mineral amendments and water. So living with these soils is best managed by increasing soil organic carbon levels and therefore moisture retention such that the drying is not as pronounced (=less cracking) and the wetting less poorly structured and sticky. So in pastoral situations keeping plenty of residual dry matter following each graze is important as is managing a base of perennials. The fastest and most cost-effective way to build soil organic carbon is by establishing and grazing mixed species pasture/cover crops. This is regardless of the soil type. Composting and other forms of organic matter application are relatively expensive however are recommended if your context can provide for it. Subsoiling on its own can lead to the cracks being less 'prismic' as they will tend to now follow the subsoiler lines. Is there a benefit? Yes and no and again its only because we often find that people don't combine subsoiling with other soil improvement strategies that we see as being necessary to support the work that subsoilers do.

2017-08-21 23:00
If you are looking to have pasture then you will need some effective fencing. No point in Morocco otherwise. Furthermore if the site has been burned then its likely that you will need to get some fertility back into the site. I'd consider therefore sowing a oat/vetch crop that's undersown with a perennial/annual pasture mix. How many sheep will depend solely on the carrying capacity of the landscape ? ask around what an average oat crop yields for example. In simple terms it will be the quantity of forage divided by the daily ration needed by the sheep. If its low quality then they'll need more and if its higher quality they'll need less. If you worked off of 4% of body weight/per day in feed you'd be in the ball park. Once you get started you can then look at using @'s gut fill and manure score metrics to refine things whilst monitoring the whole way.
