2017-07-06 23:18
@ If you go back into the Week 1 webinar recording, fairly early on Darren talks about the use of the Keyline scale of permanence from P. A. Yeoman's work, and the development of the Regrarians platform based off of that. So while intuitively or from a different perspective, it may make sense to have the Soil layer close(r) to the Climate, Geography (and Water) layers, soil is relatively easy for us to have an effect on through management practices (i.e. not a lot of effort, and soils can change relatively quickly). In contrast, Climate and Geography are nearly impossible for us to change (although we've done a pretty good job considering...), AND have enormous effects on determining the later decisions you make. The converse is also true -- changes in soils (and related aspects covered in that layer) are relatively easily reversed through management practices, whereas a change in Climate, Geography, or other "higher level" aspects, not so much. Similar reasoning for Economy... we don't want to make final decisions on what enterprises to develop until we're sure we've made a plan that fits the landscape (rather than fitting the landscape to the enterprises we want). Does that make sense? @

You can also deselect all but the climate you're interested in/located at, and find exactly where else on the planet that climate occurs. **EDIT** Comparing the two shows the huge differences between the two (little surprise -- 0.1 degree lat/long versus 5 m resolution).
