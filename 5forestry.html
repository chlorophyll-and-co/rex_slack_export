Thanks @southerndeeproots ? the first thing I would do is to create an inventory of all of the species that you have and then establish their various uses. That will include looking at their durability (in and above ground), timber/lumber qualities both in the round and dimensioned, firewood etc. When you do that then you can look to measure the individuals and start to target them for their ultimate end uses. Some are going to be ready to go now for fencing and it may be part of your silvicultural management. Otherwise the best preservation treatment is to start with a species that's already known to be durable. If that's not the case then charring is the easiest method ? then you are talking other nastier treatments such as creosote and CCA etc.

Hi @blakehudson you have got a good list going. One I could add is called Cratylia argentea. I lived in costa rica and panama for a number of years with my wife and daughter. In my experience, tropical forages are managed differently depending on whether there is a distinct dry season or not. For instance, in southern Costa Rica, where there is a very short dry season (3-4months), farmers usually grew living fences and harvest forage as their cattle are entering a new field. it is a bit casual, I can't speak to exactly how much supplement was being supplied. However, in the guanacaste region of the north west where I lived on a ranch for two years, there is a much longer dry season and pastures actually dried out for months at a time. Here farmers would cultivate Cratylia argentea in fields from which the cattle were excluded and every year they would make "micro silos", stuffing the leaves of cratyia into plastic garbage bags and then protecting those with old rice sacks. These Micro silos were used during the really dry months to supplement the feed, keeping the cattle healthy without relying so much on the over worked pastures. This system seemed to really help ranchers in the region where I lived, they all got together to help harvest and store the feed ahead of the driest months.
