RE: Primary Land Units I was reminded just then of something both @g.pavlov and I overlooked this morning (thanks @dragan!) and indeed throughout this program. Many of your project sites will have Primary Land Unit (PLU) boundaries which extend beyond your own site. Its important that you map these as well. Therefore when you are pulling together the topographic maps for your site ALWAYS try to include all of the catchment. Please refer to 2.19 - Catchment Analysis in the #2geography chapter of the RHB. (p. 113-114) for more information.

Thanks @walt ? as I've often said #KeylineDesign is a whole farm planning system (the first ever in fact) ? the principles of which can be applied to any production landscape. "...The purpose of Keyline technique and the Keyline scale of permanence in relation to climate is to take complete advantage of all weather and climate phases that will enhance soil climate through maintenance in the soil of improved relationships in the factors of moisture, warmth and air, and by contrast, minimise to the full the disadvantages of those climatic elements that destroy, namely, flood rains, drought and fire..." (p.33, 'The Challenge of Landscape", P.A. Yeomans, 1958). The primary tool in Keyline, as I see it, is the 'Keyline Scale of Permanence' and the analysis and treatments that follow. In the analysis of the 2. Land Shape, one considers first the geography (part A of the topic this week) and then the geometry of keyline approaches to these respective land shapes. With this we, using the Regrarians Platform, look to apply part B of this week's topic ? land capability ? to then determine what the most appropriate land-uses and regeneration treatments to apply. So if you have a 20ha section of flat lands then from a Keyline Design perspective the questions will be what is the 1. Soil climate, 2. the topography of this landscape, 3. how is water an influence, 4. is it well serviced by farm roads, 5. does it have sufficient farm trees to influence the soil climate, 6. what farm buildings are there to support production, 7. is there sufficient subdivision fencing (permanent and temporary) to manage livestock phases and 8. is the soil in an optimal condition to grow the pastures and crops you wish to grow. 20ha is a reasonable quantity of land in your area and if its flat then it likely that, provided the soil climate is sufficient, you'd be able to grow a decent volume of forage which can then be rationally grazed by your neighbour's cattle. Establishing either/both a winter and summer mixed species cover crop which is undersown to perennial pastures might be something to consider. Will success in doing so be dependant on subsoiling? Possibly if there is structural compaction that's difficult for root's alone to overcome. Does the internal drainage characteristics of the soils across these flat's also warrant a cultivation/sowing geometry that's perpendicular to the contour? Would mounding help address the same question? Finally after all of this I'd probably find myself concentrating on 3 strategies ? 1. planned/rational grazing 2. mixed species cover crop undersown with perennial/annual pasture species. and if doing #2 3. doing some agronomic soil testing and add the requisite N & P to establish the crop/pasture.

@brother.beans The lowest boundary in a PLU is either the edge of the flood plain (where a primary ridge and primary valley flatten so much and give way to a plain field of deposition), the edge of a creek or the edge of a secondary valley. Landscapes are fractal, this means that in a larger scheme of things a secondary valley will be a part of a larger a unit/watershed that is made up of a greater order of adjacent ridges (the runoff/erosion component). The valley by itself is not a unit/watershed, but one of the components (the deposition component) within a unit/watershed. That being said, Yeomans strictly defined a PLU as consisting of a Primary valley with its two primary ridges on either side, a main ridge as a top boundary, and the lower boundary as I mentioned above. Think of the PLU as a watershed, because thats what it is.
