If you acquire the land, you include a valuation of the timber as part of your offer (e.g. offer $2000 per acre for the timber and $500 per acre for the land to establish your basis). Neither the bank or the seller care how you value the offer as long as it meets the seller's desired sell price and it appraises at or above that price. You can then reduce your basis for the timber value as you sell the processed timber (e.g. $2000 per acre in this example). You can also depreciate 100% of the cost of the equipment in the first year under Section 179.

For input cost per bed and/ or per square meter I personally use a very simple spreadsheet with each thing you do to the crop on a square meter bases ( from the very first prep, to seed, seedling, fertiliser, minerals, irrigation(I make two different prices often, particularly with irrigation as I use fuel to drive a pump and I get rebate, so I know what the bare minimum of money I've spend on it is), all the way to the washing and boxing, row covers, cleaning up the crop, anything I can imagine. For most Items I keep two prices, one is a bare minimum one so I know somewhat when I'm making a loss, or to what price I could drop if need be. For ease of working I look at the local contractor rate for machinery work and the local price for other things like seedlings(this also gives some benchmark to become competitive in the farm's own nursery enterprise) for all work done I charge my personal rate irrespective of who does the actual job. When for eg planting or mechanical weeding I do the contractor rate plus however many staff on the implement. The contractor rate I figure should pay for the maintenance and depreciation, I know there is a large difference in running costs for different implements, but that probably balances. Anyway I wack all that stuff in the spreadsheet and then look at my minimum yields per square meter to expect work out that price per piece or kg per square meter and then I usually (depending again on a few factors like what would people be willing to pay, local area, other growers...) add another margin for the farm. It's on one hand pretty rough, but it can on the other hand have some finesse too.(Using the minimum yield means I'm less likely to have to pay for adverse conditions or whatever causes a minimum yield, ( I take already heaps of risks to provide awesome food for people, that I figure I don't want to gamble on that one too) It got me going, it might be of value for others too. I've found quickly with this approach that I can set a few base prices for just a few different 'styles' of crops with their similar treatements and make further data entry not as crucial. Also with this knowledge I then had I saw a few things that seemed quite expensive products to sell after all the margins etc. but when I sold the products(and sure they sold, slow at first though) I felt comfortable and 'righteous' to do so because of the knowledge I had gained about it. On another note I'm pleased to be moving on to more sophisticated Ag software. I'm currently going for a program called AgSquared. You've got to pay for it, but the features it has, I think warants that more than enough. We'll see how we go with it this season. Cheers

Regrarians Ltd.'s corporate structure is pretty simple ? we established a 'Public Company Limited by Guarantee' ? our accountant set this up such that the articles provide that this company is a not-for-profit (don't know the details there completely). With this we needed to nominate another non-profit organisation that, should Regrarians Ltd. become insolvent or close, any assets would be directed to ? we chose Landcare Australia in that case. Of course we're not looking to have that happen, however its one of the requirements as we understand it. This structure needs a minimum of 3 directors which works really well for us as we don't like quorum based systems ? reminds us way to much of all those committee's we've been involved in over the journey :wink: You have to come up with a 'Statement of Purpose' as well and this needs to be very well crafted in order to be clear what the boundaries of operations might be. https://www.ams.usda.gov/market-news/custom-reports is a useful USDA database on both retail and wholesale terminal pricing for both conventional and organic crops. You can get daily pricing data for a year and download it in a spreadsheet for analysis. We had a NY consultant / IT firm that wanted $15,000-$20,000 to generate reports on market pricing for use in our feasibility studies. This was their source of data. The site is slow but there are 100s of MB of data that can be downloaded. Data is available for the following markets: ? Atlanta ? Boston ? Chicago ? Dallas ? Detroit ? Los Angeles ? Miami ?
