salvador_puertorico: I am growing a few hundred fruit trees in Central Texas, in the whitetail's native range. Because of a lack of predators and restrictions on hunting, they can be a huge pressure on growing much of anything. I also use what I call cages. The cages for deer protection I build are from 2"x4" welded wire that is 5' tall and are usually around 3.5' to 5' in diameter. I use at least 3 tee posts, 2"+ bamboo canes, or rebar to keep the cages in place with wire ties. I have a set of 50 of them that I use for establishing sets of trees. The cages are only needed for the first couple years but will last for much longer than that so they can get multiple years of use. I leave the cages around the trees until the trees are tall enough that the browse height is below most of the canopy. Thanks @ and @ ? the first thing I would do is to create an inventory of all of the species that you have and then establish their various uses. 2017-08-16 00:32
Thanks @ ? I've not had a lot of experience with the management of chilean needle grass. If needle grass (Nassella neesiana), or other lower successional perennial species such as African Love Grass (Eragrostis curvula) are a component of a grassland then I'm not too worried. Its when they become dominant, which I'm sure is your concern, that we seek to apply the control levers. Its also about understanding why in a well functioning grassland does the opportunity arise for the dominance of any particular species, be it needle grass, love grass, Phalaris aquatica ('Harding Grass' in north America), or ryegrass (Lolium sp.) for that matter. The Nasellas sp. are similar in some ways to our native Stipa sp. in that they are palatable when the leaf to stem ratio is favouring leaf however this reduces significantly thereafter. Therefore its important to try and graze when its leafy and palatable and maybe hold on in there for a little longer than you would otherwise. Not so much that the livestock start to consume the surface litter, but until their dung and gut fill scores suggest that their nutritional needs are not being met. I'd be observing recoveries to see which duration of grazing periods are favouring any and all species such that you aren't making the needle grass a 'bogey-grass'. @ would no doubt suggest setting up some practice areas as would I. With regards the pigs ? I think its always wise to look to have relatively frequent moves with pigs such that there is very much a mosaic of disturbance and no more than that ? unless of course you're using them to set up a market garden or otherwise. I think with pigs too that the nutrient loading that they (and other omnivores) add to a system, by way of imported feed, requires some 'mopping up' of that nutrient in order to not waste either the opportunity it presents nor the imbalances that might otherwise favour some species over others. The best solution is to sow a heavy feeding mixed species cover crop which could then be strip grazed or stockpiled as a standing forage. This would help to march grassland succession along in favour of species other than needle grass etc.
