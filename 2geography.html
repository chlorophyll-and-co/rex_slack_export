jkbernak
2017-08-02 03:38
Hugels don't produce annual vegetable gardens off the bat. A farm on Gabriola Island on the west coast of British Columbia, Canada worked hugels into their property due to the massive amount of water received during the spring, winter and fall and upwards of 4-5 month droughts which are become more common in the west coast. The hugels being build with already decomposed wood had 10-100x surface area and thus could absorb more water via capillary action (adhesion and cohesion micro forces). This meant that for them the hugel worked to absorb winter rains better then the predominately clay soils around them and hold that moisture into the spring/summer. Also it allowed them to get into their production area a full 1 month earlier then the untreated area. Hugel provide 1) wind break and microclimate, 2) insitu composting of large cabon matter (byproduct of heat) and 3) a raise surface to work. What they do not provide is any common interface with conventional tools. If you're looking at market gardening and thinking about working anything in a production mentality or efficiency (BESIDES FULL GROWN SQUASH!) they don't work inside the larger pattern of the holy trinity of design, 1) Client Goals, Skills, Time 2) Inherent Property Attributes (Biotic and Abiotic), 3) Local Markets. I've also used hugel with tree plantings for their moisture retention. Without knowing more about your context and goals I won't come down with a prescription. Consider the objectives of what you are trying to accomplish and your context in comparison to what I've outlined here.

regrarians
2017-08-17 01:04
Best strategy I can recommend it to conduct a series of test holes around the property. A backhoe or 10+ tonne excavator are good tools for the job. Fractured gravel is likely to be found where you have metamorphosed sediments whereas basaltic rock is much harder and generally need crushing to become gravel. Granites generally need crushing too although you can get get coarse, but small diameter gravels where there's been some weathering. Sedimentary material generally needs to be metamorphosed (by heat and pressure of granites) to get hard enough to be useful as gravels. Otherwise is really only sandstones that do the job or limestone etc. Alluvial gravels can sometimes be found as conglomerate on ridges from uplifted old rivers etc. These are often smoother gravels and need to be mixed with fines (i.e. non-reactive clays) to be of use in roads. Same with gravels that you'll find in existing floodplains and water courses.
